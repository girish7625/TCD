## Users

The primary visitor is a woman in active caregiving — most often for an aging parent, sometimes for another loved one living with dementia, chronic illness, or disability. The Spring 2026 member survey (10 respondents) gives the real shape of this audience:

- **Age:** late 20s through 50s, with the 30s and 40s the dominant range. No respondents in their 60s or older.
- **Race / ethnicity:** Black, Hispanic, Asian, White, and Multiracial all represented. The community is meaningfully diverse, not a single-demographic room.
- **Geography:** heaviest on the U.S. East Coast, with West, Central, Mountain, and international members (Ireland) also represented.
- **Tenure as caregivers:** anywhere from under six months to five-plus years. Newer and seasoned caregivers sit in the same room.

She arrives on the site from a Facebook share of one of Jacquelyn's videos. The survey makes this near-total: 8 of 10 members came in from social media, 2 from word of mouth, **0 from Google search**. Google is not a meaningful front door and the site should not be designed as if it is.

She is on her phone. Mobile dominance is even larger inside the community than at the front door: 9 of 10 members access TCD on the mobile app. She is often reading late at night, in stolen minutes between caregiving tasks. She is tired in a chronic way that is its own weather.

She does not arrive cold. She has already heard Jacq's voice and trusts it. The question she lands with is no longer "is this for me?" — it is "is there really a room here, and how do I get in?"

A smaller secondary audience: people searching on her behalf (a sibling, friend, or adult child of a caregiver). Same site, same posture, no separate path.

## Product Purpose

The Care Den is a peer community for family caregivers. The community itself runs on Heartbeat, where Lynn and Jacq facilitate live sessions five to six times a week, host the between-session threads, and shape membership offers in their own voice and on their own schedule. **The community is the product. The website is not.**

**What the community actually delivers, in members' own words, is: not feeling alone.** Every one of the ten survey respondents either Strongly Agreed or Agreed with "Since joining The Care Den, I feel less alone in my caregiving experience." The recurring phrases they reach for are *feeling seen*, *being heard*, *realising how common my experience is*, *people who get it without explanation*. That last phrase is the brand promise made plain. It is the thing the site is selling — by feel, not by claim.

The website is a front porch. Its only job is to:

1. Confirm the visitor is in the right place after a Jacq video.
2. Let her feel the room — the same voice, the same care, the same posture.
3. Send her to Heartbeat to take the actual next step.

The slow surface (this website) and the fast surface (Heartbeat) are deliberately separated. The website never names a specific session, never lists prices, never announces time-limited offers, never runs experiments. Those things move at the community's pace and belong on Heartbeat. The website moves at brand pace, and stays still on purpose.

This separation is confirmed by the survey: every member-requested improvement (reminders, recordings, calendar integration, more flexible session times) is a Heartbeat-platform concern. None of them is a website concern. The front porch is correctly out of scope for what the community needs to keep iterating on.

Success looks like: a visitor arrives from a Jacq video, recognises the same voice in the first few seconds, and taps a single warm button — *Join the community* or *Become a member* — that sends her to Heartbeat. The site has done its job when it gets out of the way.

Today, the homepage receives 99.7% of pageviews, averages a 12-second session, and bounces 96% of visitors. The site brings them in — it does not yet move them through. That gap is the design problem this rebuild exists to solve.

## Brand Personality

Three words, given by Lynn and Jacq, that govern every choice:

**Roomy.** Air around everything. Generous margin, generous line-height, generous silence between elements. Nothing crowded. The reader can breathe on the page.

**Unexplained.** She does not have to perform fluency about her situation to be welcome here. No qualifying questions, no "tell us about yourself" before the door opens. The site offers itself; it does not interrogate. (Members echo this back unprompted — "people who get it without explanation" is the phrase that appears most often in the survey.)

**Kept.** Caregivers do all the keeping for everyone else. Here, she is kept. The site holds the inverse posture — careful, considered, made for her. The quality of a small press book treated as an object that deserves care.

Voice: warm, plain, direct, slow. Closer to a held hand than a pitch. No urgency. No "limited time."

## Anti-references

The site must explicitly NOT feel like any of the following:

- **Care.com / AARP** — marketplace energy, listings density, browse-providers posture. TCD is not a directory.
- **Headspace / Calm** — wellness-app gradients, glossy abstract shapes, app-screenshot heroes. Adjacent topic, wrong tone.
- **Cake (joincake.com)** — topically close (end-of-life planning) but transactional in voice. TCD is not a checklist product.

General patterns to avoid anywhere on the site: countdown timers, autoplay, popups, "limited spots available," "act now," exit-intent modals, pricing tables, FAQ accordions used as content padding, hero CTAs stacked three-deep.

## Design Principles

These are the strategic principles that govern every page, component, and copy choice. Visual rules (palette, type, spacing, motion) belong in DESIGN.md; these are the posture beneath them.

1. **One door, one direction.** The website's only job is to send the visitor to Heartbeat. It does not contain the community, list sessions, quote prices, or run time-limited offers — those live on Heartbeat where Lynn and Jacq move at the community's pace. Any feature that pulls fast-surface content onto the slow surface is wrong by construction.

2. **Honour the moment of trust.** Visitors arrive having already heard Jacq's voice (8 of 10 members come in from social media, 0 from Google). The site continues that conversation — it does not reintroduce, oversell, or convince. The reader is already three-quarters of the way in. Treat her that way.

3. **The room won't ask her to explain.** What members feel inside TCD — that they don't have to perform fluency about their situation — is how the website treats her too. No qualifying forms, no "tell us about yourself" friction before the front door, no required disclosures.

4. **Designed for the phone at midnight, with one free hand.** Mobile is the primary surface (68.6% of website traffic, 9 of 10 members on mobile inside the app). Exhaustion is the condition. Stolen minutes are the time. Every type choice, tap target, and content density rolls up to this reader. Desktop is the visiting case, not the design target.

5. **Embody, don't describe.** Roomy, unexplained, kept — the website should *feel* those words, never name them. Like Persephone Books or NYRB Classics treats a book cover: the page is the proof. If a visitor needs to read a tagline to know what kind of place this is, the design has failed.

6. **Make room for the listener, not just the seeker.** Not every visitor is ready to join. One member, asked what would help, wrote: *"Maybe finding a way to listen passively… without having to speak. Sometimes folks want to be present and rest in the presence of others."* That posture applies to the website too. Some visitors will arrive only to witness — to sit on the porch quietly and feel the room. The design must let stillness be a valid response. No "you must act," no urgency baked into the layout, no design that punishes the visitor who simply reads and leaves.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

**Floor:** WCAG 2.2 AA across the entire site.

**Tightened above the floor, anchored on the real reader:**

- **Body type:** 18–19px minimum on mobile. Line-height ≥ 1.6. Long, comfortable measure for narrative content (~65 characters). Never below 16px even for tertiary copy.
- **Contrast:** AAA on all body text. Tested on small screens in low-light conditions — the realistic reading environment.
- **Motion:** No autoplay video or audio. No motion that triggers without user action. Honour `prefers-reduced-motion` fully.
- **Interaction:** No popups, no countdown timers, no required tooltips, no hover-only affordances (the visitor is on a touchscreen).
- **Dynamic type:** Layouts must respect OS-level type scaling up to ~200% without breaking.

**What the member survey tells us:**

- A member survey was run in Spring 2026. **No respondents raised accessibility concerns.** The site should remain accessible by default, but the brief is not "solve a known access gap" — it is "keep the floor solid and don't introduce regressions."
- The improvements members did request (reminders, recordings, calendar integration, more flexible session times) are all Heartbeat-platform concerns. **The website should not try to solve them.** Doing so would violate the slow / fast separation and pull fast-surface content onto a brand surface designed to stay still.

This section should be revised if a future survey surfaces named needs.
