# The Care Den — Brand Book

*Where caregivers find each other.*

---

## How to use this book

This is the shared reference for anyone making things for The Care Den. A post, an email, an event card, a video frame. If you're building something people will see, the answers are in here.

It's built to be read in about fifteen minutes and used for a long time. The point isn't to make the work harder. It's so the whole team can hold the same line without Lynn or Jacquelyn in the room every time.

When something isn't covered here, the test is simple: does it feel like a room where someone doesn't have to explain themselves? If yes, you're probably right.

---

## 1. What we are

**The public sentence:** *Where caregivers find each other.*

**The internal sentence (how it feels from inside):** *A room where I don't have to explain.*

The Care Den is a peer community for caregivers. Not a course. Not a curriculum. A place to refill your cup, led by people who've carried the same weight. Members gather almost daily to talk, sit, laugh, vent, and breathe. Between sessions the community lives quietly, in posts, messages, and small acts of care members do for each other.

Everything we make should support that public sentence. If a headline, a caption, or a card doesn't ladder back to *"where caregivers find each other,"* it's off.

---

## 2. What we stand for

Four pillars. Not values, which tend to go generic. These describe how TCD actually shows up.

**Hold the room, don't run the program.** We host. We don't lecture. The space matters more than the agenda.

**We get it, so nobody has to explain.** Members walk in already understood. That's the whole promise.

**Move at the pace of the people inside.** No rush, no push. Come to one session or all of them. Speak or stay silent.

**Rooted in women, room open to anyone.** Built by and mostly for women caregivers, and the door stays open.

Use these as a gut check. A partnership, a session format, a campaign idea. Hold it up against the four. If it fights one of them, it's not TCD.

## 3. Voice and tone

We write the way one caregiver talks to another. Warm, plain, no performance.

**The rules:**

- Third person and warm. TCD is a place, and we speak about it and to the people in it with care.
- Plain language. No jargon, no clinical distance, no hype.
- Short sentences carry the weight. Let them.
- Never make someone feel like they have to earn their place or explain their situation.
- We don't sell. We recognize. The reader should feel seen before they feel pitched.

**Room vs. garden.** The room is the center of the brand. It's recognition and belonging, the feeling of walking in and being understood. Default to it. The garden is a second metaphor about tending, seasons, and slow growth. It's allowed in longer writing (essays, session descriptions, the newsletter, future campaigns) but it never replaces the room as the core. Don't reframe the brand around the garden.

**What we avoid:** toxic positivity, "you've got this" energy, medical or self-help jargon, urgency and scarcity ("join today!"), anything that treats caregiving as a problem to optimize. We don't cheerlead. We hold space.

**Yes / no, so the difference is obvious:**

| This sounds like TCD | This doesn't |
|---|---|
| You don't have to explain yourself here. We already get it. | Join our supportive community of caregivers today! |
| A place to refill your cup, led by people who've been there. | Leverage our peer-support ecosystem for caregiver wellness. |
| Come to one session or all of them. Speak or stay silent. | Unlock your full caregiving potential with our proven framework. |
| Some weeks we hold an expert. Some weeks we just hold space. | Expert-led workshops designed to maximize your resilience. |

## 4. Logo

The logo is the wordmark. Treat it as the quietest element on any layout. It signs the work, it doesn't decorate it.

**Clear space.** Keep a margin around the logo equal to the height of the "C" in Care on all sides. Nothing crowds it.

**Minimum size.** Don't shrink it past the point where the wordmark is comfortably legible. When in doubt, larger.

**Color.** Use den green on light grounds (porch cream, warm white). Use warm white or porch cream on den green grounds. Don't render it in lamplight, don't add gradients, don't outline it.

**Don't:** stretch it, rotate it, recolor it outside the approved pairs, add a drop shadow, place it on a busy illustration where it competes, or rebuild it in a different font.

*Confirm exact construction against the live `logo.svg` in the repo before producing print sizes.*

## 5. Color

The core palette is small on purpose. A restful, lived-in set that lets the illustrations and the writing do the talking.

| Name | Hex | Where it goes |
|---|---|---|
| Porch cream | `#f4efe6` | Primary background. The default ground for almost everything. |
| Porch cream deep | `#eee6d8` | Secondary background, subtle section shifts, cards on cream. |
| Warm white | `#fbf7f0` | The lightest ground, for breathing room and clean space. |
| Body ink | `#26211c` | Body text and most type. Warm near-black, never pure `#000`. |
| Den green | `#3a7d3f` | The signature. Primary brand color, buttons, key accents, logo. |
| Den green deep | `#28552c` | Green text on light grounds, hovers, deeper accents. |
| Lamplight | `#efb42c` | The warm highlight. Use sparingly, like a lamp left on. Accents only, never large fills or body text. |

**Rules of thumb:** cream and warm white do most of the work. Den green is the anchor. Lamplight is a spark, not a wash. Keep body text in body ink for contrast and readability. Never put lamplight behind text you need people to read.

**The illustration palette runs wider.** The watercolors reach beyond these seven into softer blues, clay reds, and muted purples so the people and objects feel real. That's fine and intended. Every illustration still anchors back to den green or lamplight somewhere in the piece, so it reads as TCD and not as stock art.

## 6. Typography

Two families. One for display, one for everything you read.

**Fraunces** — display. Headlines, big statements, the moments that carry emotion. It has warmth and a little softness in it, which is the point. Use it at larger sizes where its character shows.

**Hanken Grotesk** — body. Paragraphs, captions, UI, labels. Clean and quiet so it gets out of the way. This is where most of the words live.

**Pairing rule:** Fraunces sets the tone, Hanken carries the meaning. Don't set long paragraphs in Fraunces, and don't set a hero headline in Hanken. One display face, one body face, no third font.

**Working scale** (mirrors the site; confirm exact rem values against `globals.css`):

| Role | Face | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| Hero / H1 | Fraunces | Large, confident, generous line height |
| Section / H2 | Fraunces | Clearly smaller than H1, still display |
| Subhead / H3 | Fraunces or Hanken medium | Bridges display and body |
| Body | Hanken Grotesk | Comfortable reading size, roomy leading |
| Caption / label | Hanken Grotesk | Smaller, quieter, often in a muted ink |

**Don't:** stretch or condense the type, use all-caps for long strings, tighten letter-spacing to cram text, or mix in a fourth typeface for "fun." The restraint is the style.

## 7. Illustration

Illustration is the house style, not photography. It's how TCD feels human without exposing real members.

**The look:** traditional watercolor. Soft washes, visible texture, a made-by-hand quality. Warm, unhurried, a little imperfect.

**Technique rules:**

- Line on wash. A loose line sits over the color, but figures never get a full hard outline around the whole shape. The edges stay soft.
- Crop the figures. Partial bodies, hands, a shoulder, a circle of people seen from above. We don't need full portraits, and cropping keeps it intimate.
- Diversity is deliberate. Vary skin tones across the set on purpose. Caregivers are everyone.
- Transparent grounds. Illustrations come with no baked-in background so they drop cleanly onto cream, green, or white.
- Anchor the color. The palette can range wide, but pull den green or lamplight into every piece so it stays unmistakably TCD.

**Making new ones:** the set is generated with a fixed prompt and the hex palette above, then checked against these rules before it's used. This process is meant to be handed to the team so anyone can grow the library without breaking the style. Keep the prompt and palette in the repo next to the illustrations so they travel together.

## 8. Layout and spacing

The layouts breathe. Empty space isn't wasted, it's the calm.

**The one hard rule:** no invented background shapes or decorative fills. No blobs, no random circles, no gradient swooshes to "fill" a corner. Only two things earn space on a layout: approved illustration and type. If a spot feels empty, that's usually correct. Let it be empty.

**Spacing rhythm:** the site runs on Tailwind's spacing scale (a 4px base), so keep asset spacing on the same rhythm. Consistent, generous gaps between elements. When choosing between tight and roomy, choose roomy.

**Grounds:** start almost everything on porch cream or warm white. Reserve den green grounds for moments that want weight (a strong statement card, a closing frame).

**Hierarchy:** one clear focal point per asset. A headline, or an illustration, or a single quote. Not all three shouting at once. If everything is emphasized, nothing is.

**Alignment:** pick a grid and hold it. Ragged, casual placement reads as sloppy, not warm. The warmth comes from the color and the words, not from crooked layouts.

## 9. Putting it together (marketing assets)

Quick recipes for the assets the team makes most. Same DNA every time: cream ground, one focal point, room to breathe, den green or lamplight to sign it.

**Quote card.** A member quote or a TCD line, set in Fraunces, on porch cream. One small watercolor (a heart, a hand) anchoring a corner. Body ink text, lamplight or den green as the single accent. Logo small at the bottom with full clear space.

**Event / session promo.** Session name in Fraunces, the practical details (day, time) in Hanken. A watercolor vignette that fits the session's mood. Keep the details easy to scan. This is one of the few places function leads.

**Essay teaser.** The garden metaphor is welcome here. Essay title in Fraunces, a line of pull-copy in Hanken, a reading or gathering illustration. Warmer and slower than a promo. It's an invitation to read, not a CTA.

**Video frames (Jacquelyn's content, 9:16 vertical).** A small logo watermark placed consistently. A lower third for name and title in Hanken. Open and close bookend cards in Fraunces on cream or green. The watermark is quiet, the content is the star.

For all of these: if you're unsure, strip a thing out rather than add one.

---

## 10. Quick do / don't

**Do:** start on cream, let it breathe, one focal point, den green to anchor, lamplight as a spark, Fraunces for the moment and Hanken for the meaning, crop the illustrations, keep the writing plain and warm.

**Don't:** invent background shapes, fill every corner, use lamplight behind text, set paragraphs in Fraunces, add a third font, recolor the logo, sell with urgency, or make anyone feel they have to explain themselves.
